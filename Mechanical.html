Motorized door mechanism

To achieve the door actuation we used a belt and pulley system to rotate the acrylic doors about the hinges. In order to achieve this we needed to keep the doors lightweight or else the motors in our mechatronics kits would not have enough torque to rotate the doors.

We also wanted the door gap at the top to be about 6 mm since a bigger gap would allow users more access to forcing the doors open with their hands, defeating the purpose of our hands-free system. This is why we integrated the pulleys into the top hinges which can be seen in the images below.

The pulleys needed to be printed accurately to ensure the belt won't slip during power transmission. We were able to achieve this accuracy by using a Markforged Mark II printer

Manufacturing

The overall structure is made out of acrylic plastic. We decided to laser cut this portion of the project because the structure was a simple geometry and laser cutting would provide the fastest, cheapest, and accurate results compared to other manufacturing techniques like CNC and 3d printing.

Ch allenges

The biggest challenge I faced was coming up with a design that would address all the needs in our problem statement. I also struggled with creating an actuation method for closing and opening the door, even though I was ultimately able to come up with a unique design that addressed all our needs, I still found that there were many improvements that could be made (such as using thicker material for the walls, or a servo motor in place of a stepper motor), however I would say given the teamwork and outcome, this project was a success.

Lessons Learned

I learned a lot from this project, especially regarding teamwork. I have not always had the best luck when it comes to group projects at SFSU but working with this team has been a breath of fresh air. Working with this group taught me how to trust my group members to take care of their tasks and communicate with them in a way that keeps us efficient and productive. I also learned a lot about integrating electronic components into mechanical devices which is an important tool for a Mechanical Engineer to have under their belt in the technological age. I look forward to taking the skills I have learned/developed from this project and applying them to obstacles outside of the campus walls.

Challenges

The biggest challenge from this project was the short amount of time we had from the design phase to presenting the project at the COSE showcase. Typically the projects I worked on had longer time frames which I was used to. Another challenge was working with the foam insulation, acrylic, and integrating the FSR sensors to get consistent input from the sensor

Lessons Learned

Better time management and role management was huge because of the short amount of time we had to complete this project. When I was cutting the foam I was using a box knife and this would make a mess of the foam. I remembers seeing machines using wires and heat to produce clean and accurate cuts in foam and this inspired me to heat up the box knife when I was cutting the foam. This work much better and produced a better prototype. Finally, I learned that FSR sensors are very sensitive and can detect the smallest amounts of pressure. I wasn't able to implement a way to correct this problem but if I had more time I would have integrated small springs to take the pressure off the FSR when there is nothing on the foam.
